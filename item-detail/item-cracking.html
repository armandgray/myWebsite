Before The Interview

  • Getting Experience

    Landing a job starts first and foremost with gaining the right experience. For students, this means taking those big CS project classes and finding relevant internships that can you coding as much as possible. For the rest of us, this means starting projects. Don't worry about what to work, whether it will be impressive or relevant. The most important part of getting experience is diving into the code, starting to write code early.

  • Great Resumes

    Great experience leads to great resumes. Your resume is the key to opening all the closed doors. Resumes should be a single page detailing your employement history, project, skills and education with strong bullets. Common mistakes for resumes is attempting to jam all your experience into an 8 1/2 x 11, when you should be highlighting the most critical components of your work for the recruiters 10 second read. Strong bullets are the key to strong resume and should present a meaurable result of an action you took: "Accomplished X by implementing Y which led to Z [some measurable outcome]."

  • Making a Plan

    "Failure to make a plan is planning for failure." This old saying holds true in coding just like anywhere else. Gaining great experience, creating a resume, preparing for interviews, etc. All of these step take time and effort and can be heavy with stress and fatigue without a plan. Start early and plan your path. CtCI contains a great "Preparation Map" on page 30.

The Interview Process

For coding jobs, the interview process can be the scariest step even after getting through the door, but this section is here to help you be as best prepared as possible for success. It's important to understand that the interview process is an assessment of your performance and ability, but generally will not result in a quantitative score. "At the end of the interview, the interviewer will walk away with a gut feel for how you did."

Your interviewers assessment will be based on the following categories: Analytical Skills (quality of solution), Coding Skills, Technical Knowledge, Experience, and Fit (culture, communication & personality).

Here are a few key points for acing an interview: Problem-solving skills are valuable, for entry-level positions, basic data structure and algorithm knowledge is required, learn work on whiteboards and think aloud and understand that interview questions are a general "free for all" given the needs of the company and team to which you are applying. That being said, the vast majority of CtCI covers real interview quetions & solutions, which provide a general roadmap for understanding interview questions at different levels.

Specific Situations

For Startups  --  The proces is "highly variable," but most focus is on passion & initiative. Demonstrate you can "hit the ground running" and entrepeneurship. Top jobs by referral.

For Big Inc.  --  Usual pattern: two or three phone screens (technical and sometimes with shared document editor), and an on-site interview. Companies vary most on their assessment or feedback process for final decisions.

  • Google  --  1-2 phone screens. 1st screen with engineer (tough technical questions). On-site with 4-6 people measuring "Analytical Ability, Coding, Experience, and Communication. Special: offers require 1 "enthusiac endorser".

  • Apple  --  2-4 phone screens. 1st screen for basic skills, then technical screens with team members. On-site with 6-8 people & a mix of 1-on-1 and 2-on-1 interviews (expect to whiteboard). Special: Excellent Skills & passion for position and Apple.

  • Facebook  --  1-2 phone screens. Screens with tough technical questions & and coding via shared document editor. On-site with 3-4 people with roles: Behavioral, Coding & Algorithms (usually 2 people), and Design/Architecture. Special: Seeking entrepeneurship & ability to "hack together elegant and scalable solutions."

For Interviewers  --  Good interview training makes the difference between finding good candidates, letting them slip through the cracks and hiring bad engineers unintentionally. Aim to ask questions with several engineering hurdles to get multiple data points. "Use hard questions, not hard knowledge & avoid 'scary' questions" (eg: Math or Probability, low-level knowledge, and proprietary systems).
